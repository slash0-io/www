faq

The questions a careful platform team should ask.

what happens if slash0 disappears tomorrow?

Nothing breaks. This is the question to ask any vendor in your security path, so here are the actual mechanics: the prefix lists live in AWS and your rules reference them directly - if every slash0 system went dark, your security groups keep enforcing the last-published ranges indefinitely. Nothing expires, nothing fails closed, no traffic stops. Ranges would gradually go stale, exactly as if you'd hand-maintained them and stopped. And ejecting is trivial: copy the list's entries (they're plainly visible in your console) into a prefix list you own, repoint your rules, done. No lock-in mechanism exists to remove.

what does slash0 see about my environment?

No traffic, no data, no credentials - we hold no access to your account. AWS shows a prefix-list owner two things about consumers: which account IDs accepted the share, and which security-group IDs reference each list. We use that solely to refuse revoking a share you still depend on. Details on the security page.

what does it cost?

The feed and Terraform provider are free, permanently. That's the point of them. The hosted tier is free for design partners during early access; paid plans arrive at general availability, priced per account-region well under the cost of running a single managed-firewall endpoint. (For scale: AWS Network Firewall starts around $288/month per AZ before you've written a rule or processed a byte.)

which regions?

Prefix lists are regional, so we publish per region. Early access starts in us-east-1; additional regions are enabled per design-partner need. Tell us yours in the early-access request.

IPv6?

Yes. Prefix lists are per-address-family in AWS, so every purpose ships as up to two lists - egress.stripe.api.v4 and, where the vendor publishes v6 ranges, .v6. Your v4 and v6 rules reference them separately, matching how security groups treat the families.

how fast do updates propagate?

Typically inside the hour of a vendor publishing, and within a minute of the change landing in our feed. Removals are slower on purpose. A range the vendor drops stays in the list through a grace window, so old and new ranges overlap during vendor rotations and your traffic never gets caught between them.

how does the quota math work?

Referencing a prefix list consumes the list's max_entries against your rules-per-security-group quota (default 60). That is an AWS mechanic, not ours. We do three things about it: size max_entries tightly (you pay quota for the max, not the actual), compute the exact quota your subscriptions need at onboarding and file the Service Quotas request in the same CloudFormation stack, and publish every purpose's rule cost in the catalog so there are no surprises.

what about services behind Cloudflare or CloudFront?

IP pinning is the wrong tool for them, and we say so: pinning a shared CDN range allowlists the whole CDN, including every attacker-controlled site behind it. The catalog classifies every service (dedicated / mixed / cdn-shared) so you know before you build. A later phase adds hostname-aware enforcement, in your VPC, for exactly these services.

who runs slash0?

An independent engineer who got tired of the Lambda-updates-a-prefix-list contraption every serious team rebuilds from scratch. Small on purpose: the entire delivery mechanism is AWS primitives, the pipeline is open source, and the design goal throughout is that you shouldn't have to trust us more than you can verify.
