how it works

From a vendor's publication to your security group, and everything that can refuse along the way.

the feed

Everything starts with the open-source feed pipeline. For every service in the catalog we track the vendor's official publication - their JSON endpoint, their documented ranges page - never a third-party aggregator. Each rebuild:

  • Records provenance. Every service document carries the upstream URL, retrieval timestamp, and SHA-256 of the exact body it was derived from.
  • Aggregates losslessly, never widens. Duplicate and subsumed ranges are removed; adjacent exact halves are merged. The covered address set is preserved exactly - CI proves it. We never summarize across gaps, because overshoot space inside shared cloud ranges is rentable by any attacker.
  • Refuses suspicious data. A purpose that parses to zero ranges fails the build. A change that removes most of a service's previously published ranges is quarantined: the last-good version keeps serving while a human reviews.
  • Publishes signed. The feed index carries an ed25519 signature; every service document is hash-chained to it. Verify it yourself.

The result is a versioned, signed JSON feed at feed.slash0.io/v1 with sync tokens and a public changelog of every real change. The free Terraform provider and the hosted tier both consume this same artifact - there is no privileged data path.

delivery

The hosted tier publishes each purpose as an AWS customer-managed prefix list in a slash0-operated publisher account - one list per service, purpose, and address family (egress.stripe.api.v4, egress.datadog.agents.v6) - and shares them to your accounts via AWS RAM. Shared lists are read-only to consumers and referenced in security group rules exactly like AWS's own managed lists.

Onboarding is one CloudFormation stack in your account: it accepts the RAM share invitation and files the rules-per-SG quota increase your subscriptions need (a referenced list draws its max_entries against that quota - we compute the number, you approve the stack). From then on, a reconciler compares the signed feed against every published list, every minute, and converges them.

Nothing of ours is in your data path. Your traffic flows through your security groups to the vendor, as before. If every slash0 system went dark, your rules would keep working exactly as-is - see "what if slash0 disappears?"

five refusals between a mistake and your traffic

The dangerous moment for any managed-list product is a bad update propagating everywhere at once. Five independent layers each get a chance to refuse:

layerwhat it catches
1 · feed guardrailsTruncated or erroneous vendor publishes - zero-range builds fail; mass removals are quarantined while last-good keeps serving
2 · signature verificationTampered or unsigned feed data - the publisher verifies ed25519 + hash chain before applying anything
3 · publisher guardrailAn update that would start removing most of a serving list freezes that list and pages a human - independently of layer 1
4 · canary-first rolloutOur own consumer account references every published list and takes every change first
5 · grace windowsRemoved ranges keep serving for a grace period - old and new coexist, so vendor rotations are non-events; ranges a vendor re-publishes mid-grace are restored, not churned

And beneath all five: prefix lists version natively in AWS, so rollback is a one-call restore - not custom machinery.

where pinning stops

IP pinning only works for services with dedicated, published ranges. The catalog classifies every service - dedicated, mixed, or cdn-shared - and when a CDN-fronted service can't be pinned safely, it says so instead of selling you a leaky allowlist. (Pinning a shared CDN range allowlists the entire CDN, including every attacker-controlled site behind it.) Those services are the target of a later phase: hostname-aware egress enforcement that runs entirely in your VPC, built on this same catalog.
